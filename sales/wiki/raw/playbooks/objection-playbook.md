# Sales Objection Playbook — 100xEngineers Applied AI Cohort

## Overview
This playbook covers the 5 most common objections reps encounter when selling the 100xEngineers 6-month Applied AI cohort. Each objection has 3 response modes depending on where the lead is in their decision process.

---

## Objection 1: "It's too expensive."

**What they really mean:** They haven't yet connected the value to their specific situation. Price is the surface objection; belief gap is the real one.

### Mode A — Warm (curious, early stage)
"What would need to be true for this to feel like a no-brainer investment? Let's start there."
Then map their answer to the cohort's outcomes. Don't defend the price — reframe around the return.

### Mode B — Pressure (pushed back hard)
"I hear you. Let me ask — what does one year of staying where you are cost you? Not just money — time, opportunity, the gap between where you are and where you want to be?"
Then pause. Let them answer. Their own answer does the work.

### Mode C — Direct (just needs numbers)
"The cohort is [price]. But let's put that in context: the average engineer who completes this gets a 30-40% salary bump or a new role within 6 months. On a ₹12L CTC, that's ₹3.6-4.8L/year return. The math works. What's making you hesitant?"

**Never:** Offer a discount in the first objection. It signals the price was never real.

---

## Objection 2: "I'm not the right time / I'm too busy."

**What they really mean:** They're not convinced this is urgent. Busy is a prioritization signal.

### Mode A — Warm
"That makes sense. Can I ask — when does busy let up? And when it does, will you actually have more time to invest in this, or will something else fill it?"
Most people realize they're always busy. Urgency comes from them, not you.

### Mode B — Pressure
"The IT wave happened whether people were ready or not. The ones who acted in the first 2 years are now the ones managing teams of 10. The AI wave is in year 1. Busy didn't protect the people who sat out the IT shift."

### Mode C — Direct
"The program is designed for working professionals — 8-10 hours/week. If you can find 1.5 hours a day, you can do this without quitting your job. What specifically would need to change in your schedule?"

**Never:** Accept "not the right time" without exploring what the right time looks like. Open-ended follow-up questions surface whether this is real or a polite no.

---

## Objection 3: "I need to think about it / talk to my family."

**What they really mean:** Either they have an unexpressed concern, or they genuinely need buy-in from a spouse/parent. Both are valid — treat them differently.

### If it's an unexpressed concern:
"Of course. Before you go — is there anything specific you're unsure about that we haven't covered? Sometimes the thinking-about-it stage goes faster when we've addressed the actual question."

### If it's a genuine family decision:
"Absolutely. What would help your [spouse/family] feel good about this? Should I send you something you could share with them? Or would a quick call with them be useful?"

Then send a clear, non-pressured summary: what the cohort covers, the time commitment, the cost, and 2-3 success stories from relevant backgrounds.

**Follow-up timing:** 3 days max. WhatsApp, not email. Keep it warm: "Hey — just checking in. Did you get a chance to chat with [family]? Happy to answer any questions they have."

---

## Objection 4: "I'm not technical enough."

**What they really mean:** They think this is for engineers. It isn't.

**The experience reframe:**
"The cohort isn't about becoming a programmer. It's about understanding AI well enough to direct it, work with it, and build on top of it. Think about what a product manager does — they don't write code, but they understand it well enough to lead engineers. That's what we're building here."

Domain-specific angles:
- **Designer:** "Your aesthetic judgment + AI tools = output that would take a junior dev team 3 weeks, in 3 days."
- **Marketer:** "You'll run campaigns that would require a 5-person team with the AI stack we teach. Your output multiplies, not your workload."
- **Doctor/Healthcare:** "The first wave of AI health tools are being built by tech people who don't understand medicine. You do. That's the gap the market is paying for."
- **Founder:** "Non-technical founders who understand AI are now building products that were impossible without a co-founder 2 years ago."

---

## Objection 5: "I can learn this on YouTube / ChatGPT / free resources."

**What they really mean:** They're comparing structured learning to self-directed learning. Help them see the difference.

**The accountability and cohort angle:**
"You absolutely can learn this on YouTube. The question is whether you will — and whether what you learn will be connected enough to build something real. This cohort gives you 3 things free resources don't: structured progression from diffusion → LLMs → agents, live project work you can put in your portfolio, and a cohort of peers who are doing the same thing and will push you."

**The curation angle:**
"The problem with free resources isn't availability. It's signal-to-noise. The cohort is 6 months of distilled curriculum from people building production AI systems. You could spend 6 months on YouTube and still not hit the depth we cover in module 2."

**The output angle:**
"What does learning on YouTube produce? Notes. This cohort produces a portfolio of AI projects you've shipped."

## Quick Reference: Objection → Mode Selector

| Signal | Use Mode |
|--------|----------|
| Still asking questions, curious | A (Warm) |
| Resistant, repeating the objection | B (Pressure) |
| Analytical, wants specifics | C (Direct) |
| Said "I'll think about it" twice | Uncover the real objection first |

---

## The Meta-Rule
Every objection is a question in disguise. The question is: "Why should I believe this is worth it for me, specifically?" Answer that question — with their domain, their situation, their timeline — and the objection dissolves.
