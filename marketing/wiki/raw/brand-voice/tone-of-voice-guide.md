# Tone of Voice Guide

> Version 1.2 | Last updated: 2026-05 | Owner: Brand Lead

---

## What This Guide Is For

Every sentence we write is a brand decision. This guide defines how we sound so that regardless of who's writing — social copy, an email subject line, a product announcement, a job description — it sounds like us.

This is not a grammar guide. It's a perspective guide. It answers: what do we believe, how do we say it, and what do we never say?

---

## 1. Brand Voice in Four Words

**Honest. Sharp. Warm. Direct.**

Not clever for clever's sake. Not corporate for safety's sake. We sound like a person who knows what they're talking about and respects the intelligence of the person they're talking to.

---

## 2. Voice Pillars

### Honest
We say what we mean. We don't hide behind jargon, vague claims, or passive voice. If something has a limitation, we say so. Trust is built on clarity, not polish.

- **In practice:** "This works well for X, but isn't designed for Y" vs. "This is an all-in-one solution"
- **Honest ≠ brutal:** We're direct, not dismissive. We respect the audience.

### Sharp
We value our reader's time. Short sentences over long ones when both work. One idea per paragraph. The right word, not the safe word.

- **In practice:** "It failed because we miscalculated load" vs. "There were some unforeseen technical challenges that impacted performance"
- **Sharp ≠ terse:** We still give context. We just don't pad it.

### Warm
We're a team of people building things with other people. We don't talk down. We don't treat customers as conversion events. We notice the human.

- **In practice:** "Thanks for flagging this — we missed it" vs. "We have received your feedback and will investigate"
- **Warm ≠ sycophantic:** No hollow affirmations. No "great question!" No "absolutely!"

### Direct
We say the thing. We don't build up to the point. We lead with the news, add context after.

- **In practice:** "The launch is delayed by two weeks. Here's why and what's next." vs. "We wanted to take a moment to share some important updates about our upcoming product launch timeline..."
- **Direct ≠ blunt:** We explain the why. We just don't bury the lede.

---

## 3. Writing Rules

### Lead with the point
Put the most important information first. Every email subject line, headline, and opening sentence should tell the reader what matters before anything else.

### Sentence length
Mix it. Long sentences have their place — building an argument, adding nuance. But the most important things get short sentences. One word, even. Sometimes.

### Active voice, always
"We fixed the bug" not "The bug has been fixed." "You'll get access" not "Access will be granted to you."

### Numbers and specifics over generalities
"3x faster" not "significantly faster." "200,000 users" not "a large user base." "Ships February 14" not "coming soon."

### Contractions
Use them. "We're" not "we are." "It's" not "it is." Sounds like a person, not a document.

## 4. Channel Adaptations

### Website (hero / product pages)
- Short, punchy headlines (5-9 words ideal)
- Lead with benefit, not feature
- Body: medium sentences, clear structure
- CTAs: action verbs ("Try it free", "See how it works", "Start building")

### Email (campaigns + transactional)
- Subject lines: specific and honest — no clickbait
- Body: conversational, structured with clear visual hierarchy
- Sign-off: from a named person when possible
- CTAs: one per email, clear and single-minded

### Social (LinkedIn, Twitter/X, Instagram)
**LinkedIn:** Long-form performs. Share a specific insight or decision — not a press release. 1 post per week, not 5.
**Twitter/X:** One sharp take. A thread when you have 5+ connected points. No hot takes for engagement.
**Instagram:** Visual-first, caption supports the image. Don't describe what they can see.

### Internal comms (Slack, Notion, emails to team)
Same voice — honest, direct, warm. Don't become a different person internally. If something went wrong, say so plainly. If something went well, say so specifically ("Dhruv closed 12 leads this week, up from 4 last week" not "great job everyone this week").

## 5. What We Never Say

### Banned phrases
| Phrase | Why | Say instead |
|--------|-----|------------|
| "Exciting announcement" | Every announcement is called exciting | Just make the announcement |
| "Thrilled to share" | Hollow, PR-speak | "We're shipping X" |
| "Game-changing" | Overused, unearned | Describe the actual change |
| "World-class" | Empty superlative | Name the actual quality |
| "At the end of the day" | Filler | Delete it |
| "Moving forward" | Avoids accountability | Say what you're actually doing |
| "Reach out" | Corporate | "Email us", "message us", "call us" |
| "Leverage" (as a verb) | Jargon | "Use" |
| "Synergy" | Never | Never |
| "Circle back" | Vague | Set a specific time |
| "Per my last email" | Passive aggressive | Address the issue directly |

### Do not do
- Don't use exclamation marks more than once per piece of content. One is fine for genuine excitement. Multiple signals a lack of confidence in the words themselves.
- Don't use all-caps for emphasis. Use bold, or restructure the sentence.
- Don't hedge everything. "This might potentially help you" → "This helps with X."
- Don't apologize for existence. "Sorry for the long email" → just write a shorter email.

---

## 6. Tone Calibration by Situation

| Situation | Tone |
|-----------|------|
| Product launch | Sharp + Direct (benefit-first, specific, confident) |
| Incident / outage | Honest + Direct (what happened, what we did, what's next) |
| Customer support | Warm + Honest (acknowledge, own it, fix it) |
| Hiring | Warm + Sharp (real about the role, specific about the team) |
| Thought leadership | Sharp + Warm (specific take, human voice, no hedging) |
| Apology | Honest + Warm (no corporate non-apology language) |

---

## 7. The Test

Read it aloud. Does it sound like a real person talking to another real person? Would you say this to a colleague you respect?

If the answer is no — rewrite.
